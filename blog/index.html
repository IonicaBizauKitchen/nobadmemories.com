No Bad Memories

Text editor nostalgia A few weeks ago, I posted this ten-minute quickstart video on VS Code, a new open-source code editor from Microsoft. Making the video got me thinking a bit about nostalgia for text editors. Yeah.

For those of you who are web developers, what was the first tool you used to make websites? Was it Notepad? I mean, good old Notepad, right? This barebones text editor has been shipping with Windows for decades and has essentially no features whatsoever for web development. But it was free and got the job done. Its lack of features were actually point of pride back in the late 1990s and early 2000s. After all, being able to create a website in Notepad meant you knew how to write code without the aid of specialized software like WYSIWYG ("what you see is what you get") visual editors. And that was something to brag about.

When I started making websites at the age of 12 or 13, I did indeed start with Notepad. I also have a faint memory of using some WYSIWYG editors—perhaps CoffeeCup, Macromedia Dreamweaver, and FrontPage—but I don't remember much about them. The first text editor that I really felt a certain pride or affinity for was NoteTab Light. I loved NoteTab Light! It allowed you to have multiple documents open as tabs! This feature might seem unremarkable to anyone who has used a modern browser, but this was a truly wonderous acheivement in the late 1990s. NoteTab Light had some other good features, too, like an "Open in Browser" button. What a timesaver! It didn't have HTML syntax highlighting—that came with the paid version—but it was a glorious piece of software nonetheless.

There's something kind of charming about old text editors and the way we used editor badges like the ones above on personal websites to show our pride or to show off.

I miss the days of waving our flags in gaudy banners best viewed at 800 x 600 resolution. How about you? Are there text editors you miss? Do you feel nostalgic for some bit of unloved software from the '90s? Let me know on twitter. Oh, and you can watch my video on VS Code below.

Delete: A Design History of Computer Vapourware I recently had the opportunity to review Paul Atkinson's book Delete: A Design History of Computer Vapourware.

Delete is highly recommended for those interested in computer hardware design or retrocomputing. The volume is packed with gorgeous, never-before-seen color images of computers that were announced but never commercially produced. Atkinson's interviews with the designers and executives closest to these unrealized products are fascinating.

My in-depth review of Delete can be found in the latest issue of Design and Culture. The PDF is available for free online. You can also browse the photo collection of former Sinclair Research designer Rick Dickinson, who worked on a number of the unrealized products chronicled in Delete.
